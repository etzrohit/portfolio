Context

The feature worked. No one could adopt it.

An AI built and designed it end to end. Incident Responder is the highest-stakes feature of Praxis, Facets' AI platform engineer. My job was to drive its adoption.

The AI already worked. A usage study I ran confirmed investigations succeeded 99 to 100% of the time, once I fixed a scoring bug that was hiding it.

But adoption had stalled. Every new team needed a live call to get set up, and pilot accounts left their credits unused.

Research

How I found the problem

Adoption was low, but no one knew why. I investigated it instead of guessing. Tap any step to see how.

01 Built a usage dashboard across 23 environments and 71 customer orgs

I wrote a pipeline that pulled real usage from every environment and customer org. It showed most accounts were inactive, and surfaced a scoring bug that had been marking healthy alerts as failed investigations. Once fixed, the data was clear: the AI was succeeding 99 to 100% of the time. The model was not the problem.

02 Interviewed on-call and DevOps engineers

I talked to the people who set it up, and the ones who gave up, to hear in their own words where they got stuck. The same answers kept coming back: they couldn't get through setup, and once inside, they couldn't tell what anything meant.

03 Mapped adoption as a funnel and found the leak

I broke adoption into stages: know it exists, start setup, finish setup, connect a source, get first value, keep using it. Both the data and the interviews pointed at one leak: setup and first run, not the investigation.

04 Audited all 9 screens and ranked every issue

A heuristic review plus a first-time-user walkthrough across all 9 screens produced 52 issues, each rated P1 to P3 and placed on an impact-versus-effort matrix. That matrix became the build order.

Problem

What I found

The AI investigated well, but the product explained itself badly. New teams couldn't tell whether setup had worked, and the screens hid the answers the AI had already found. Every new team needed someone to walk them through it.

01 Nothing confirmed the setup was working

After finishing setup, a team landed on a vague screen that said "set up a trigger" or "all clear." There was no way to send a test alert or see that the Slack bot was connected, so they couldn't tell if it was working until a real incident happened, if it ever did.

02 The incident list hid everything behind a click

Every incident was a collapsed row. The severity, the AI's summary, and the action buttons were all hidden until you clicked each row open, one at a time. When an engineer is woken at 3am, they need to scan the whole list and triage in seconds, not expand rows.

03 The AI's answer was buried under five cards

The investigation, which is the whole point of the product, rendered as five stacked cards with a scroll inside a scroll. The root cause and the recommended fix sat below the fold, and there was no way to copy them into Slack during an incident call.

04 One product went by four different names

The same feature was called "Praxis SRE" in the sidebar, "Incident Management" in the header, "Incident Responder" at setup, and "ai-sre" in the chat link. One first-time user asked, "What's ai-sre? I thought this was the Incident Responder." Nothing told them these were all the same product.

Prioritization

Why now

High value, low effort. The impact-versus-effort matrix made the order obvious.

Value

High

The highest-stakes Praxis feature, already pushed to pilot accounts. Blocked adoption meant unused credits and churn risk.

Effort

Low

The AI already worked, so nothing was riding on model changes. This was UX and onboarding, and most of the highest-impact fixes were small, self-contained UI changes.

How I chose them

I did not track raw usage. A real incident forces a team into the product anyway, so page visits prove nothing. Instead I measured two things: could a team finish setup without help, and did people actually reach the root cause once they were in an incident.

Decision

The two calls I made

Two decisions shaped the work: how to ship it, and how to keep it consistent long after I moved on.

Call 1: ship in small pieces, worst pain first

Considered

One big redesign

Land the whole new experience in a single release.

Why not: it was slow and risky on a feature customers were actively evaluating, and no team gets any improvement until the whole thing ships.
Chose

One fix, one small pull request

Order the work by the impact-versus-effort matrix and ship each fix on its own.

Why it won: the fixes that helped most and cost least shipped first, like a readable incident list and a test alert that confirms setup. Teams felt the difference in days, and each change was small enough to review and ship on its own.

Call 2: build a design system, not one-off screens

Why I built shared components

Each screen in this feature was generated by AI on its own, so no two looked or worked the same. Fixing them one at a time would not stop the next new screen from being just as inconsistent.

So instead of patching screens, I built the parts they all share:

  • The pieces that repeat: connection cards, the alert-filter editor, status badges, empty states, and drawer headers.
  • One set of patterns and one product name, used on every screen.

Now a new screen starts from these parts and matches the rest by default, even the ones the AI generates. The team keeps one consistent product without redoing this work each time.

Solution

What I changed

I rebuilt three parts of the product: onboarding and setup, connecting alert sources, and the incident list and investigation.

1/3 Onboarding design

Onboarding and setup

  • Setup used to drop teams on generic screens with no clear starting point. Now a guided workspace setup connects a Facets environment, a cloud account, or Kubernetes.
  • Nothing confirmed setup had worked. Now a "setup complete" message appears once triggers and sources are wired.
  • There was no way to check the connection. Now a Test action on each trigger fires a sample alert to confirm it works.
  • The old landing screen showed nothing useful. Now a dashboard shows open and critical incidents, time to acknowledge, time to investigate, top services, and root-cause mix.
Setup
Teams finishing setup without help 28%82%2.9×
2/3 Configuration design

Triggers and sources

  • Alert sources were each their own separate screen. Now one place lists where alerts come from: Slack, webhooks, Teams, or Mattermost, each with its own setup flow.
  • There was no way to filter alerts, so simple ones that never needed the AI were triggering full investigations and burning credits. Now every trigger has an alert filter to control which alerts get an investigation.
  • It wasn't clear what the AI could read to investigate. Now sources make it explicit: a Facets environment, Kubernetes clusters, or a cloud account.
3/3 Information & interaction design

The incident list and investigation

  • Every incident was hidden behind a click-to-expand. Now every row shows severity, the AI's root-cause summary, a confidence label, status, and actions in place.
  • Duplicate alerts flooded the list and the filter dropdown reset each time. Now duplicates group into one incident, with status and severity filters that stay applied.
  • The answer sat below five stacked cards. Now the investigation leads with the summary, root cause, and fix, with the evidence in tabs.
  • Acknowledge and Resolve were buried in a dropdown. Now they're buttons, with copy-to-Slack and the incident's lifecycle and cost alongside.

Reflection

What I'd take forward

Worked well

  • I confirmed the AI itself was solid before touching anything. That let me point the whole redesign at the experience, instead of second-guessing the model.
  • The screens were the symptom. Fixing the shared system underneath is what makes the consistency last.

Would do differently

  • Build the shared components before the third screen, not after. I ended up rebuilding a few parts twice.

The lesson

  • AI can build a feature. Making it adoptable still takes product judgment.
