01

What Facets is

  • Running a company's software on the cloud (AWS, Google, Azure) normally needs a DevOps specialist and a lot of manual setup.
  • Facets lets a regular developer set it up themselves, safely and the same way every time.
  • 30+ companies run their live products on it, all through one web app. That app is what I redesigned.
02

The problem

  • The same menu showed on every screen, whether you were at the org level, inside a project, deep in an environment, or below. Nothing told you where you were. The root cause was structural, not visual.
  • Two separate interfaces had been running in parallel for about a year.
  • Everyday tasks took too many clicks and a lot of hunting across screens.
  • The app didn't show what was happening. A release ended on a blind confirm, with no preview of what would change.
  • The product had too many levels to move through, so it was easy to lose track of where you were.
  • Inconsistent patterns and no dark mode made the product feel unfinished.

Same left menu, every level, from the projects list to deep inside a project.

03

The evidence

Customer interviews

  • Small, frequent tasks took too many clicks. Advanced users wanted one place to do their daily work.
  • Developers were stuck with DevOps settings in the nav they don't use and can't access.
  • They couldn't find what they needed on a screen, so they dropped off mid-flow to go cross-check elsewhere.

Usage data (Mixpanel)

  • Most tasks needed jumping across several screens to finish, like the release flow and resource config overrides.
  • The main overview pages (environment, resource) had low traffic, even though they should be where people start.
04

Why now

  • The app was already being rebuilt on new technology, for reasons that had nothing to do with design.
  • Every screen was going to be touched anyway.
  • Fixing the experience now cost very little extra. Fixing it later meant a second full pass.
05

Business goals

  • Reduce confusion throughout the product.
  • Fix the navigation, so people always know which level they're on and how to move between org, project, and environment.
  • Bring transparency to key flows, so people can see what's happening and trust it.
  • Reduce the number of clicks to complete everyday tasks.
  • Reduce support tickets (how-to and enhancement requests).
  • Retire both legacy interfaces and move every customer onto one.
06

The decisions

1
Navigation model. We compared Environment-first (a top switcher rewrites the side nav beneath it) with Context-switch (each level keeps its own steady nav). We chose Context-switch, because it matches how engineers already picture the hierarchy: project, then environment, then resource.
2
Design system. We decided not to build our own. We adopted Ant Design and built on design tokens, customizing only where it fell short, so our time went to the hard flows instead of a button library.

11 Solution · 5 of 5

The rest of the redesign

What we did
  • Kept the flows similar and simple.
  • Created consistent patterns across flows: release, CI/CD, images.
  • Built on design tokens, powering full dark mode.
  • Handled accessibility throughout: contrast, keyboard, and focus states.
  • Enhancements that help daily work: compare secret and variable values across environments, easy rollback, one-click release debugging, and a better blueprint.
Goals checked
  • Reduce confusion
  • Retire both legacy interfaces
Impact
  • One consistent, accessible product across every flow
  • 70% moved to dark mode in 30 days

In closing

What I learned

  • The real fix was the structure underneath, not the screens. Getting the navigation model right was the whole project.
  • Shipping the code myself, as PRs the engineers reviewed, kept the design intent intact from Figma all the way to production.
  • Measure the right thing: for flows people can't avoid, measure friction; for optional pages, measure whether they come back.
